Harry Fletcher-Wood is a former history teacher who has worked at a number of school improvement organizations in the UK. His blog Improving Teaching has been a great resource for usable classroom- and school-level practices. This post is about a practical approach to school improvement. Grand plans almost never come to fruition. Schools operate in complex social, political, and economic spaces. Too many factors are outside of the control of educators, building leaders, or even system leaders.

Harry borrows a program from the Harvard Center for International Development. Small foreign aid programs are typically at the whim of complex local and regional forces. They are most successful when they are narrowly focused on local high-will problems and iteratively chip away at shared problems. This approach builds a self-sustaining improvement capacity. The authors also explore how local adaptive improvement work can actually support developing governance capacity in weak systems. This makes me wonder if we could adapt some of these ideas to help struggling schools and districts to build their self-governance capacities from the ground up. 

What makes Problem-Driven Iterative Adaptation different?

The hallmarks of this approach ‚Äì known as Problem-Driven Iterative Adaptation (PDIA) ‚Äì are:
  • Finding local solutions for local problems: don‚Äôt copy best practice, work with local teams to solve the problems which matter most to them
  • Encourage positive deviance: encourage experimentation and find people who are solving the problem already
  • Iterate and adapt: experiment, review, adapt
  • Scale through diffusion: create champions who ensure the change is viable, legitimate and relevant (Andrews et al., 2015, 125)
Further reading

The team advancing Problem-Driven Iterative Adaptation have shared their work generously. I particularly recommend:
  • This paper: an accessible overview of the approach and its merits
  • This fascinating (free) book extending the argument, by explaining how capability traps preventing development, and how they can be addressed.
  • The PDIA Toolkit, which offers invaluable guidance in putting it into practice.
