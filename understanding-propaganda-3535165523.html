The Harvard EdCast just released a new podcast on Propaganda Education for a Digital Age. The guest, Renee Hobbs, director of the Media Education Lab at the University of Rhode Island, describes education around understanding propaganda was a part of ELA instruction until the rise of the standards movement in the 1990s. At that point, the standards shifted to focus on argumentation - messages based on logic - rather than messages based on emotion. Hobbs notes that only a small fraction of schools cover propaganda at all. When they do it is almost always focused on Nazi Germany and for a handful of schools, propaganda associated with WWI. 

In addition to not providing students with the tools to identify and understand appeals to emotion, this gap in instruction also ignores positive propaganda. Collective action - both negative and positive - routinely relies upon emotional arguments to focus on the greater good. From our own work, we know from experience that evidence - the logical argument - is never enough, we need to be able to connect to our and our clients' emotions to deliver real change. 

I really enjoyed this refresher on propaganda. You might as well.
